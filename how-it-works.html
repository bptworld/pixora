How it Works

The server runs on your own system. Your displays pull cards from your network, not ours. In fact we don't even have any servers.

It works because you own the engine.

The server, card schedule, settings, and display content run from your own system. Cloud services can add convenience, but they are not the foundation holding the whole thing up.

1

Start the local server

The control center runs on your computer. It stores your settings, card queue, device list, and the content each display should show.

2

Connect your displays

Each display joins your Wi-Fi and checks in with the server on your local network. Your setup stays inside your home or workspace.

3

Cards keep running

The server renders and schedules the cards, then the displays pull what they need. Weather, clocks, dashboards, scores, and custom cards stay in motion.

4

You stay in control

If an outside service changes, slows down, or disappears, your local setup still has the core pieces it needs to keep showing cards.

Other services rent you the lights. This keeps the switch in your hand.

Many connected displays depend on a company server to decide what appears next. Here, your own machine is the source of truth, so your cards are not stranded if a cloud dashboard goes away.

Why local-first matters

The difference is not just technical. It is the difference between owning a working system and depending on someone else's server to keep your display alive.

No shutdown cliff

If the company behind the product changes direction, the core card rotation can keep running because it is served from your system.

Your data stays close

Settings, devices, schedules, and card content are managed locally, reducing the need to send everything through a remote service.

Cloud when useful

Online APIs can still feed individual cards, but the display system itself is not locked behind a mandatory cloud account.

Built to keep glowing.

A local server means the experience belongs to you: your cards, your displays, your schedule, running from your own machine.
